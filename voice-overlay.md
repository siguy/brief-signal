## Tone

**Write like a senior seller briefing their team over Monday coffee.** Not a marketing email. Not a product pitch.

- **Insider, not outsider.** We know this market. We're in the rooms. Frame things from our seat at the table.
- **Practical over promotional.** Every "Your angle" should be something you'd actually say in a meeting, not something from a slide deck.
- **Technically credible.** We're expected to be "technically fluent." Don't dumb things down — but connect technical details to business outcomes.
- **Honest about the landscape.** If a competitor shipped something impressive, acknowledge it and pivot to our opportunity: "This validates the category we're building in."
- **Numbers tell the story.** Use specific data points. Google Cloud leads with numbers (60%+ gen AI startups, 97% retention, 20x Vertex AI growth, 4M+ Gemini developers).

## Section-Specific Guidance

### "Your angle with founders:"
Write these as things you'd actually say in a 1:1 meeting. Test: would a rep feel comfortable saying this out loud to a Series A founder?

**Good:** "Are you locked into a single model provider, or do you want optionality? We're seeing the smartest founders keep their options open."
**Bad:** "Google Cloud's Model Garden offers 200+ models for enterprise-grade deployment."

### "Conversation starter:"
These should be genuine questions that open a discussion, not leading questions that pitch.

**Good:** "Giga ML is closing DoorDash with 5 people. What would your company look like if you could 20x your output?"
**Bad:** "Have you considered how Google Cloud's AI platform could help you scale?"

### "Try This Week"
Make this something the team can actually do this week. A question to ask, a demo to show, an article to forward to a founder.

## What NOT to Do

- Don't turn the briefing into a product pitch. It's market intelligence first.
- Don't mention Google Cloud in every section. 2-3 natural touchpoints per edition is plenty.
- Don't claim Google Cloud is better without evidence. Use positioning, not assertions.
- Don't ignore impressive competitor moves. Acknowledge and reframe.
- Don't use corporate marketing language ("leverage," "synergy," "digital transformation"). Write like a human.
